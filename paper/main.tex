\begin{abstract}
World models that tokenize visual observations with Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) inherit a structured codebook where each code maps to a point on a discrete coordinate lattice.
Standard cross-entropy training ignores this structure, treating all incorrect predictions as equally wrong.
We introduce \emph{Structured Label Smoothing} (SLS), a training objective that distributes smoothing mass according to a Gaussian kernel over FSQ coordinate distances, weighted by per-dimension perceptual sensitivity.
We integrate SLS into a full Vision-Model-Controller pipeline for the platformer game Geometry Dash, combining an FSQ tokenizer, a causally-masked transformer with Adaptive Layer Normalization and 3D Rotary Position Embeddings, and an MLP controller on the transformer's hidden state, trained with PPO entirely in dreamed rollouts.
The resulting system runs at 30\,FPS on consumer hardware.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ===========================================================================

Learning world models that predict future observations from actions is central to model-based reinforcement learning~\citep{ha2018world}.
Recent approaches tokenize visual frames into discrete codes and train autoregressive transformers to predict token sequences~\citep{micheli2023iris, hafner2025dreamerv3}.
Finite Scalar Quantization~\citep[FSQ;][]{mentzer2024fsq} offers a simple alternative to VQ-VAE codebooks: each latent vector is independently rounded to one of $L$ levels per dimension, yielding $\prod_d L_d$ implicit codes with no codebook collapse and no auxiliary losses.

A key property of FSQ, largely unexploited in downstream tasks, is that its codebook has a natural coordinate structure.
Each code maps to a point on a $D$-dimensional integer lattice, and the distance between two codes in this space is semantically meaningful: codes that differ by one step in one dimension represent visually similar patches.
Yet standard cross-entropy training treats all incorrect predictions as equally wrong, discarding this metric information entirely.

Label smoothing~\citep{szegedy2016rethinking} is a well-known regularizer that replaces hard targets with a mixture of the ground truth and a uniform distribution over classes.
We observe that for FSQ-tokenized world models, the uniform assumption is suboptimal: a token one lattice step away from the target is a near-miss, while a token at the opposite corner of the codebook is a gross error.
We propose \emph{Structured Label Smoothing} (SLS), which replaces the uniform component with a Gaussian kernel over FSQ coordinate distances, calibrated by per-dimension sensitivity weights derived from empirical patch reconstruction error.

This can be viewed as part of a broader shift from data-agnostic heuristics to structure derived from learned representations, analogous to how learned strided convolutions replaced fixed max-pooling for spatial downsampling~\citep{springenberg2015striving}.
Uniform label smoothing treats the label space as structureless; SLS recovers the metric that the quantizer has already learned.
Unlike knowledge distillation, which requires a separate teacher model to produce soft targets, SLS exploits geometry already present in the FSQ codebook at no additional cost.

We evaluate SLS within a complete Vision-Model-Controller (V-M-C) pipeline for Geometry Dash, a fast-paced platformer that demands frame-accurate predictions under a 33\,ms latency budget.
Our system consists of three components: (1)~an FSQ tokenizer that encodes $\imgsize$ Sobel edge maps into $\gridsize$ grids of discrete tokens, (2)~a $\nparams$-parameter causal transformer with Adaptive Layer Normalization (AdaLN-Zero) for action conditioning~\citep{peebles2023dit} and QK-norm for attention stability~\citep{esser2024sd3}, and (3)~a lightweight MLP controller trained via PPO entirely in dreamed rollouts~\citep{schulman2017ppo}.
The full pipeline achieves real-time inference at 30\,FPS on consumer hardware.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Structured Label Smoothing (SLS)}: a training objective that exploits FSQ coordinate geometry to distribute smoothing mass according to codebook distance, generalizable to any discrete latent space with a coordinate metric.
  \item \textbf{A real-time world model system}: a complete V-M-C pipeline for Geometry Dash running at 30\,FPS, combining FSQ tokenization, AdaLN-conditioned transformers, and PPO-in-dreams.
  % TODO: Add quantitative contribution once ablations are done.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}
% ===========================================================================

\paragraph{Discrete world models.}
\citet{ha2018world} introduced the Vision-Model-Controller paradigm, encoding frames with a VAE, modeling dynamics with an RNN, and training a controller in dreamed rollouts.
IRIS~\citep{micheli2023iris} replaced the VAE with a discrete tokenizer and the RNN with an autoregressive transformer, showing that discrete tokens enable sample-efficient learning.
DreamerV3~\citep{hafner2025dreamerv3} demonstrated that discrete representations with symlog predictions generalize across diverse domains, and \citet{hafner2025dreamer4} scaled this approach further.
TWISTER~\citep{burchi2025twister} added action-conditioned contrastive predictive coding (AC-CPC) to improve temporal coherence.
Our work builds on these ideas but focuses on a previously unexploited axis: the structure of the discrete codebook itself.

\paragraph{Finite Scalar Quantization.}
FSQ~\citep{mentzer2024fsq} replaces learned codebooks with per-dimension rounding to fixed levels, eliminating codebook collapse and commitment losses.
Each code is a point on a $D$-dimensional integer lattice, giving the codebook a natural coordinate structure.
Prior work has used FSQ as a drop-in replacement for VQ-VAE but has not leveraged this coordinate structure during training.

\paragraph{Label smoothing.}
\citet{szegedy2016rethinking} proposed replacing one-hot targets with a uniform mixture to reduce overconfidence.
While effective as a regularizer, uniform smoothing ignores class relationships.
Knowledge distillation and soft-target methods distribute mass based on teacher predictions, but require a separate teacher model.
We instead derive the target distribution from the codebook geometry itself, requiring no additional model.

\paragraph{Adaptive conditioning.}
FiLM~\citep{perez2018film} introduced adaptive affine conditioning via scale and shift parameters.
DiT~\citep{peebles2023dit} applied this as AdaLN-Zero with zero-initialized gates for stable training of diffusion transformers.
\citet{esser2024sd3} added QK-norm (RMSNorm on queries and keys per head) to stabilize attention logits in deep AdaLN models.
LeWorldModel~\citep{maes2026leworldmodel} applied AdaLN to world model transformers for robot manipulation.
We adopt AdaLN-Zero with QK-norm for action conditioning, finding that QK-norm is essential for training stability (Section~\ref{sec:adaln}).

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{FSQ tokenization (Vision)}
\label{sec:fsq}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

We encode $\imgsize$ grayscale Sobel edge maps using an FSQ-VAE with levels $\fsqlevels$, producing $\vocabsize$ implicit codes.
The encoder applies three stride-2 convolutional blocks with residual connections, projecting to a 4-channel latent map of size $\gridsize$.
Each spatial position is independently quantized using the iFSQ bounding function~\citep{vali2026ifsq}: $2\sigma(1.6z) - 1$ in place of $\tanh(z)$, scaled to half-levels and rounded, yielding $\ntokens$ tokens per frame. This substitution achieves near-uniform bin utilization without modifying the codebook size or training procedure.
The decoder mirrors the encoder with transposed convolutions.

Training minimizes MSE reconstruction loss with two regularizers: temporal slowness~\citep{huh2023grwm} ($\lambda_\text{slow} = 0.1$) encouraging smooth latent trajectories across consecutive frames, and a uniformity loss~\citep{wang2020uniformity} ($\lambda_\text{unif} = 0.01$) preventing representational collapse.
The FSQ-VAE achieves full codebook utilization ($\vocabsize / \vocabsize$ active codes) with a perplexity of 475 out of $\vocabsize$ (76\%). Perplexity is defined as $\exp(H) / V$ where $H$ is the entropy of the empirical code distribution; full utilization (100\% active codes) means every code is visited, while 76\% perplexity indicates near-uniform usage (a perfectly uniform distribution would yield 100\%).

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Structured Label Smoothing}
\label{sec:sls}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Motivation.}
Standard label smoothing~\citep{szegedy2016rethinking} replaces a one-hot target $\mathbf{e}_i$ with:
\begin{equation}
  q_\text{uniform}(j \mid i) = (1 - \varepsilon)\,\delta_{ij} + \frac{\varepsilon}{V - 1}(1 - \delta_{ij}),
  \label{eq:uniform_ls}
\end{equation}
where $V = \vocabsize$ is the vocabulary size and $\varepsilon$ controls the smoothing strength.
This distributes $\varepsilon$ uniformly across all alternatives, ignoring codebook geometry.

\paragraph{FSQ coordinate distance.}
An FSQ codebook with levels $\mathbf{L} = (L_1, \dots, L_D)$ assigns each code $i$ a coordinate vector $\mathbf{c}(i) = (c_1(i), \dots, c_D(i))$ on a $D$-dimensional integer lattice.
We define the weighted squared distance:
\begin{equation}
  d^2(i, j) = \sum_{d=1}^{D} \alpha_d \left( c_d(i) - c_d(j) \right)^2,
  \label{eq:fsq_dist}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_d$ are per-dimension sensitivity weights.

\paragraph{Per-dimension calibration.}
To determine $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$, we measure how much a one-step perturbation in each FSQ dimension affects the decoded image.
Over 5{,}000 validation frames, we perturb each dimension $d$ by $\pm 1$, decode both the original and perturbed codes, and compute the resulting reconstruction MSE relative to the unperturbed baseline.
The weights $\alpha_d$ are set proportional to these empirical sensitivities, normalized to mean 1, with 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals over frame subsampling.
This yields $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = \fsqdimweights$ for our $\fsqlevels$ codebook, revealing a mild per-dimension anisotropy (dimension 2 is the most robust, dimension 3 the most sensitive) even on the uniform-level codebook.

\paragraph{Gaussian kernel smoothing.}
We replace the uniform distribution in Equation~\ref{eq:uniform_ls} with a Gaussian kernel over FSQ distances:
\begin{equation}
  w(i, j) = \exp\!\left( -\frac{d^2(i, j)}{2\sigma^2} \right), \quad j \neq i,
  \label{eq:gaussian_kernel}
\end{equation}
yielding the structured smooth target:
\begin{equation}
  q_\text{SLS}(j \mid i) =
  \begin{cases}
    1 - \varepsilon & \text{if } j = i, \\[4pt]
    \displaystyle \varepsilon \cdot \frac{w(i, j)}{\sum_{k \neq i} w(i, k)} & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:sls}
\end{equation}
The full target matrix $\mathbf{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times V}$ is precomputed once and stored on GPU (Figure~\ref{fig:sls_kernel}).
We use $\sigma = \fsqsigma$ and $\varepsilon = \fsqeps$.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/sls_kernel.pdf}
  \caption{SLS target distribution for token 312 (coordinates $(2,2,2,2)$). \textbf{(a)}~2D slice over dimensions 1--2 with dimensions 0 and 3 fixed at the target's values: weights decay as a Gaussian with FSQ lattice distance. Red square marks the target token. \textbf{(b)}~All 624 alternative tokens sorted by SLS weight (log scale) vs.\ uniform label smoothing. SLS concentrates mass on near-miss tokens while suppressing distant ones.}
  \label{fig:sls_kernel}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Integration with focal loss.}
To handle class imbalance (background tokens dominate over obstacle and spike tokens), we combine SLS with focal loss~\citep{lin2017focal}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{SLS-focal} = -\sum_{j=1}^{V} (1 - p_j)^\gamma \, q_\text{SLS}(j \mid i) \, \log p_j,
  \label{eq:sls_focal}
\end{equation}
where $p_j = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_j$ and $\gamma = \focalgamma$.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Transformer world model (Model)}
\label{sec:transformer}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The world model is a causal transformer that predicts the next frame's tokens given a context of $K = \nctx$ past frames and their associated actions.

\paragraph{Sequence construction.}
Each frame contributes $\ntokens$ visual tokens (from the $\gridsize$ FSQ grid) plus one status token (ALIVE or DEATH), for 65 tokens per frame.
A context window of $K + 1$ frames (including the target) produces a sequence of $(K+1) \times 65 = 325$ tokens.
All target-frame tokens are replaced with a learnable \texttt{[MASK]} embedding to prevent information leakage.

\paragraph{3D Rotary Position Embedding.}
We apply factored RoPE~\citep{su2024rope} encoding three positional axes: spatial row, spatial column, and temporal frame index.
The head dimension (64) is partitioned into three bands (20 row, 20 column, 24 time) with separate base frequencies ($\theta_\text{spatial} = 10$, $\theta_\text{time} = 10{,}000$).
Status tokens receive the grid center coordinates $(3.5, 3.5)$ to represent whole-frame summaries.

\paragraph{Block-causal attention.}
Within each frame block, attention is bidirectional (tokens attend to all positions in the same frame).
Across frames, attention is strictly causal: each frame attends only to current and past frames.

\paragraph{AdaLN-Zero action conditioning.}
\label{sec:adaln}
Rather than inserting action tokens into the sequence, we condition the transformer on the action history via Adaptive Layer Normalization~\citep{peebles2023dit, perez2018film}.
The $K$-step action sequence is one-hot encoded and projected through an MLP to produce per-layer scale, shift, and gate parameters that modulate each sublayer:
\begin{equation}
  \text{AdaLN}(\mathbf{x}) = (1 + \mathbf{s}) \odot \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{b}, \qquad
  \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{g} \odot f(\text{AdaLN}(\mathbf{x})),
  \label{eq:adaln}
\end{equation}
where $f$ is the attention or MLP sublayer and $(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{g})$ are predicted from the action context.
Unlike standard AdaLN-Zero~\citep{peebles2023dit}, which initializes gates to zero, we set gate bias~$= 1$ to preserve residual flow at initialization; we found that zero-initialized gates suppress both conditioning and learning in this setting (Section~\ref{sec:training_stability}).

\paragraph{QK-norm.}
We apply per-head RMSNorm to queries and keys before computing attention scores~\citep{esser2024sd3}.
This prevents attention logit overflow that otherwise causes training divergence in AdaLN-conditioned models, as we observed in our ablation of training stability (Section~\ref{sec:training_stability}).

\paragraph{AC-CPC auxiliary loss.}
Following \citet{burchi2025twister}, we add an Action-Conditioned Contrastive Predictive Coding loss.
Learned predictors map each frame's hidden representation and future actions to a shared embedding space, trained with InfoNCE~\citep{oord2018cpc} at temperature $\tau = 0.1$.

\paragraph{Output projection and total loss.}
The output projection shares weights with the input embedding (weight tying).
The total training loss is $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{SLS-focal} + \lambda_\text{CPC} \mathcal{L}_\text{CPC}$ with $\lambda_\text{CPC} = 1.0$.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{PPO controller (Controller)}
\label{sec:controller}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The controller is a lightweight MLP that takes the transformer's hidden state $\mathbf{h}_t$ as input and outputs a jump probability and a state value estimate.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The controller is a single-hidden-layer MLP ($\hdim$ hidden units with LayerNorm and SiLU), followed by zero-initialized linear actor and critic heads. Input is the transformer's hidden state $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{\hdim}$; output is a jump probability and a state value estimate. The full controller has $\ctrlparams$ parameters.

This design follows the TWISTER~\citep{burchi2025twister} and DreamerV3~\citep{hafner2025dreamerv3} pattern where the controller operates on the latent state produced by a trained world model. Our $\mathbf{h}_t$ already encodes spatial and temporal structure through \nlayers{} transformer layers with 3D-RoPE and AdaLN action conditioning, so an additional encoder on the token grid would duplicate work already done upstream.

\paragraph{Training in dreams.}
The controller is first pretrained via behavioral cloning on expert demonstrations, then fine-tuned with PPO~\citep{schulman2017ppo} in dreamed rollouts.
At each iteration, 512 rollouts of up to 45 steps are generated by autoregressively sampling from the frozen world model.
Rollouts terminate when the predicted death probability exceeds 0.5.
Rewards are $+1$ per survival step with a $-0.2$ penalty per jump to discourage gratuitous jumping (an idle agent dies immediately upon reaching an obstacle, while a jumping agent survives longer by spending time airborne, creating a deceptive local optimum).
We use GAE~\citep{schulman2017ppo} with $\gamma = 0.995$, $\lambda = 0.95$, and clip ratio $\varepsilon_\text{PPO} = 0.2$.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ===========================================================================

\paragraph{Environment.}
Geometry Dash is a rhythm-based platformer where the player controls a cube that moves at constant horizontal speed.
The only action is a binary jump.
Colliding with any obstacle causes instant death.
Levels are deterministic: the same action sequence always produces the same outcome, but frame-accurate timing is required.

\paragraph{Data collection.}
We collect 4,265 gameplay episodes (252K frames) including both intentional-death episodes (covering diverse failure modes) and 36 expert demonstrations (full level clears).
Vertical shift augmentation ($\pm 2, \pm 4$ pixels) expands the dataset 5$\times$.
Frames are preprocessed with Sobel edge detection and resized to $\imgsize$.

\paragraph{Training details.}
The FSQ-VAE is trained for 200 epochs with Adam (lr $= 10^{-3}$, batch size 2,048) on frame pairs.
The transformer is trained for 200 epochs with Adam (lr $= 4 \times 10^{-3}$, weight decay $0.01$, batch size 512) using cosine annealing with 5-epoch warmup.
We apply 5\% random token replacement and 5\% FSQ-neighbor noise (perturbing a random FSQ dimension by $\pm 1$) as data augmentation.
Death frames are oversampled $4\times$ via weighted sampling.
BF16 mixed-precision training is used throughout; BF16 requires no loss scaling and is numerically more stable than FP16.
All training runs on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.

\paragraph{Training throughput.}
We benchmarked 11 training configurations (all combinations of \texttt{torch.compile} mode and floating-point precision) using subprocess isolation to prevent CUDA allocator fragmentation across runs.
All \texttt{torch.compile} modes are within 3\% of each other on our workload (default 6.42$\times$, reduce-overhead 6.48$\times$, max-autotune 6.55$\times$ over eager FP32).
We adopt \texttt{reduce-overhead} with BF16 (314\,ms per gradient step, 6.48$\times$) as the default: the additional ${\sim}1\%$ offered by \texttt{max-autotune} does not outweigh its occasional CUDA-graph autotuning failures on new model code.
This configuration is applied to the FSQ-VAE, transformer, and controller training scripts.
While the A100 was occupied with a primary training run, a secondary NVIDIA RTX 2060 SUPER (Turing, SM 7.5) was used in parallel to train auxiliary networks and probe augmentation / architecture variants, requiring its own hardware-specific optimization stack.
Turing lacks native BF16 (software-emulated, ${\sim}12\times$ slower than FP32 on this card) and has no TF32 support (Ampere+ only); additionally, Inductor's channels-last layout heuristic triggers a stride assertion in the convolution backward, which we disable by setting \texttt{torch.\_inductor.config.layout\_optimization = False}.
Under these constraints, the best-performing local configuration is \texttt{torch.compile} in \texttt{default} mode with FP16 and a \texttt{GradScaler}, yielding a 2.08$\times$ speedup over eager FP32 on the same FSQ-VAE kernel.

\paragraph{BC pretraining ablation.}
To verify that behavioral cloning pretraining is a convergence accelerator rather than a stability requirement, we train one PPO run from random initialization.
Cold-start PPO converges to the same plateau as BC-initialized PPO on all metrics (eval survival, entropy, jump ratio) but requires ${\sim}2{,}000$ additional iterations (${\approx}6$ hours on A100); BC itself takes ${\sim}5$ minutes, giving a 72$\times$ compute ROI.
BC is retained in the pipeline on this basis.

\subsection{Training stability of AdaLN variants}
\label{sec:training_stability}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

% TODO: Turn this into a proper figure/table once all runs are final.

During development, we ran five AdaLN configurations to identify the minimal set of stabilization techniques required:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No gates} (4 params/layer): CPC reached 0.171 before diverging at epoch 44 due to attention logit explosion.
  \item \textbf{Zero-init gates} (6 params/layer): CPC plateaued at 0.276 with overfitting. Zero-initialized gates suppressed all residual flow, killing both conditioning and learning.
  \item \textbf{Gate bias $= 1$}: CPC reached 0.189, diverged at epoch 46. Restored learning speed but unbounded attention scores persisted.
  \item \textbf{Tanh bounding + gate bias $= 1$}: CPC reached 0.205, still diverged. Bounding modulation values to $[-1, 1]$ proved that the explosion source was not in the modulation parameters.
  \item \textbf{QK-norm + gate bias $= 1$}: Stable through 200 epochs, CPC $< 0.14$, surpassing all previous configurations. Per-head RMSNorm on queries and keys eliminated the attention logit overflow that was the true root cause.
\end{enumerate}

This progression demonstrates that in AdaLN-conditioned world model transformers, attention stability (via QK-norm) is more critical than modulation bounding.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ===========================================================================

Our evaluation is limited to a single game (Geometry Dash) with a binary action space and deterministic level layouts.
The generalization of SLS to environments with higher-dimensional actions, stochastic dynamics, or RGB observations remains to be validated.
The per-dimension calibration of $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ requires a one-time empirical measurement that may not transfer across different FSQ configurations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ===========================================================================

% TODO: Write after results are finalized.
We introduced Structured Label Smoothing, a training objective that exploits the coordinate geometry of FSQ codebooks to improve next-token prediction in discrete world models.
Integrated into a full V-M-C pipeline with AdaLN-conditioned transformers and PPO-in-dreams, the system achieves real-time 30\,FPS gameplay on Geometry Dash.
SLS is in principle applicable to any model that predicts over a discrete latent space with a coordinate metric.
A natural extension is to make the kernel bandwidth $\sigma$ and per-dimension weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ learnable and jointly optimized with the world model, moving SLS from a data-derived prior to a fully end-to-end objective; care must be taken to prevent degenerate solutions in which the learned targets collapse to uniform smoothing.
