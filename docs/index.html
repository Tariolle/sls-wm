Abstract

Discrete world models tokenize observations with a learned quantizer and predict next-frame tokens with a transformer. Standard cross-entropy training treats every incorrect prediction as equally wrong, discarding a signal that Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) makes available by construction: each code sits at a point on an integer coordinate lattice, so some wrong predictions are near-misses (one quantization step away in one dimension, a near-identical reconstruction) while others are gross errors (opposite corner of the codebook, unrelated content).

We introduce FSQ-Structured Label Smoothing (SLS), a training objective that replaces uniform label smoothing with a kernel over the integer-lattice coordinates of the FSQ codebook, so a near-miss prediction is treated as a near-miss rather than a gross error. We train the FSQ encoder jointly with the dynamics transformer entirely in the discrete latent space: prediction gradients flow back into the encoder through a learnable linear projection and a straight-through estimator, and a light pixel-reconstruction loss is retained as an anti-collapse regularizer on the encoder, not as a predictive objective.

We study three kernel families (Gaussian, Laplace, Cauchy) under this joint-training setup and use an isotropic kernel with bandwidth set by a first-neighbour rule, which requires zero data-derived hyperparameters. We evaluate on Geometry Dash, a deterministic platformer with binary actions and precision timing, where the controller is trained entirely in imagination and deployed at 30 FPS on the real game via screen capture.

Pipeline overview

Structured Label Smoothing

Uniform label smoothing distributes mass equally across the vocabulary, treating all incorrect tokens as equally wrong. SLS replaces the uniform component with a kernel over FSQ lattice coordinates, so tokens one quantization step away from the target receive most of the smoothing mass and distant tokens receive negligible weight.

Method highlights

  • Joint-embedding training. A single online FSQ encoder embeds both context and target frames into a shared discrete latent space; the transformer predicts future tokens zt+1 from past tokens z≤t in that space, with no pixel-prediction head.
  • Prediction-gradient routing. A learnable linear projection sits between the encoder's continuous latents and the transformer input; it contributes zero in the forward path (discrete indices still drive the transformer) but, under a straight-through estimator, carries prediction gradients back into the encoder.
  • Anti-collapse anchor. A light pixel-reconstruction loss is retained only as an anti-collapse regularizer on the encoder. Removing it causes the joint encoder + transformer to degenerate. Finding a decoder-free discrete anti-collapse signal (a discrete counterpart to SIGReg) is open work.
  • Zero-calibration kernel. The SLS bandwidth is set by a first-neighbour rule on the FSQ lattice, so no per-dataset sensitivity probe is needed and the hyperparameter is robust to codebook drift under joint training.
  • Real-time deployment. The full V-M-C pipeline runs at 30 FPS on consumer hardware, driving Geometry Dash in real time via screen capture with GPU-resident token buffers, CUDA Graphs, and torch.compile in reduce-overhead mode.
